xG Model - Accuracy and Goodness-Of-Fit

In the first part of this series we constructed a simple expected Goals-model solely relying on two predictors: the distance and angle from goal for each shot. As you remember, our model past the eye test, i.e. it maps shot locations to xG-values that make intuitive sense to us. In this post we want to evaluate the quality of this model more formally with tidymodels’ yardstick package.

As a reminder, a visualization of our xG-estimates from the first part of this series.

Our modeling problem is not much different to the classic ML application of classifying spam and non-spam emails. With an xG-model we try to predict the outcome of a shot based on properties associated with it (location, build-up, body part); For a spam-classification model you try predict if the email is unwanted spam based on properties associated with the email (sender, subject, certain words, language, time sent). Even though we’ll see that xG modeling with existing data available is vastly harder than spam classification I will gro through the common steps of evaluating the goodness-of-fit for classification models.


As a first step to evaluate the goodness-of-fit we can compute the accuracy of our model, i.e. how often does our model classify a shot correctly as a goal or non-goal. For this we need to make a decisions about the threshold we want to use for this prediction. For which xG values do we want to make the prediction that this shot will likely to results in a goal? An obvious starting point would be 0.5. This may initially sound strict as this will only predict goals for shots within the 6 yard box, but choosing a value lower (and further from the goal) is like betting against an unfair coin. This would also repeat the mistake of pundits predicting that any shot within the 18 yard box is a sitter.

xg_pred <- predict(xg_fit, test_data, type = "prob") %>% 
  bind_cols(test_data) %>%
  rename("xG" = ".pred_1") %>%
  mutate(is_goal_pred = factor(if_else(xG > 0.5, 1, 0), levels = c("1", "0")))

xg_pred %>% 
  metrics(truth = is_goal, is_goal_pred) %>%
  select(-.estimator) %>%
  filter(.metric == "accuracy") 
## # A tibble: 1 x 2
##   .metric  .estimate
##   <chr>        <dbl>
## 1 accuracy     0.893


An accuracy of 89.3% sounds pretty good at first, but we need to bring this into the context of our data. The shot/goal data is a highly class-imbalanced data set meaning that there is a significant disparity between the number of positive (goal) and negative labels (no goal). If 89% of all shots do not results in a goal our model is no better than a model with no explanatory power at all but always predicting no goal.

To shine some more light on this question let’s look at the confusion matrix of our model predictions. And indeed, of our 10569 shots in our test data, 9395 or 88.9% did not result in a goal. Solely judged on this metric our model is not better than no model.

xg_pred %>% 
  conf_mat(truth = is_goal, is_goal_pred) %>%
  pluck(1) %>%
  as_tibble() %>%
  ggplot(aes(Prediction, Truth, alpha = n)) +
  geom_tile(show.legend = FALSE) +
  geom_text(aes(label = n), colour = "white", alpha = 1, size = 8)


To further zoom in on this point, let’s look at precision (What proportion of positive identifications was actually correct?) and recall (What proportion of actual positives was identified correctly?). You may have already noticed in the confusion matrix that we have a problem here: out of the 1174 goals we only predicted 150 correctly (12% recall) and out of our 260 goal predictions only a bit more than half were correct.

tibble(
  "precision" = 
     precision(xg_pred, is_goal, is_goal_pred) %>%
     select(.estimate),
  "recall" = 
     recall(xg_pred, is_goal, is_goal_pred) %>%
     select(.estimate)
) %>%
  unnest(cols = c(precision, recall))
